Japanese pop culture part 1：
物の怪

Mononoke

The Japanese Yokai culture has been passed down to this day and has formed a unique system. Even in modern times, Yokai is still active in Japanese culture. In ancient Japan, when someone did something that didn't make sense, they would think that monsters were causing trouble. Over time, these monsters were given names and forms and became anthropomorphic.

Kappa is a legendary creature in Japanese mythology. It has the beak of a bird, the limbs of a frog, the body of a monkey, and the shell of a tortoise, like a complex of various animals. Legend has it that his weakness is the dish above his head. As long as he tricks the kappa to bend and let the water in the dish above his head run out, he will lose his energy. In many modern works, Kappa is usually portrayed as a cute creature or ghost. Through Japanese writer Akutagawa Ryunosuke’s brilliant pen, Kappa (published in 1927) is no longer a terrible image of a water ghost, but instead has become a humorous typical figure that can lead people into a magical utopia. world.

The monsters are not only derived from nature, but some are derived from man-made objects. The daily necessities depicted in this period are also a watershed in the history of Japanese culture. The purpose of these simulant monsters is to alert the world: treat objects well, otherwise they will imitate the human way of life and take revenge on mankind.
