"Japanese pop culture part 5: "
"Miku Hatsune"

Miku

"Miku Hatsune represents Japanese pop culture as she was originally created in Japan and gained her initial popularity in Japan. She can be considered pop culture due to the mass amount of media that is centered around her.

Creation

"Miku Hatsune is the mascot for the third version of the Yamaha Vocaloid programs. She was the visual representation of the vocals that the program created. Her overarching success led to the creation of more vocaloids with mascots and expanded the bounds of what a mascot could be. Miku was created to be a 16-year-old female and was initially released on August 31, 2007. Her most recent release/update was November 27th, 2020. Miku Hatsune was never intended to be the virtual sensation that she is today. Miku was originally intended to be 'the face of computer software that allowed users to generate their own music, with her as a vessel'(Petrarca). Which is a roundabout way of saying that she was just meant to be box art. She was created to look the way she is to give the packaging a much more modern look to catch the attention of potential buyers. Miku was purposely designed to appear futuristic, as the company wanted to convey the idea that Vocaloid programs were the next tool of the future for music design."

"First_Virtual_Idol"

"While Miku is the first internationally recognized virtual idol, she isn't actually the first virtual idol. A virtual idol is an idol, aka musical preformer, who isn't a real human being.The first virtual idol was Kyoko Date, however, since she didn't become main stream she never got properly regonized as the first virtual idol. At this point you may be asking what a virtual idol is. This is important to Japanese pop culture as it shows that in order to get regonized as a crutial part of pop culture, you must first be someone/something that is instantly recognizable."

Vocaloids

"'The success of Hatsune Miku has spawned the creation of related virtual idols such as China’s Luo Tianyi'(Rouse). But her success didn't just impact other companies, it also impacted her own company. Due to Miku's success, all the new Vocaloid programs that came after her had their own character, or had a reboot character. These new virtual idols in the vocaloid franchise haven't yet reached the same level of popularity as Miku, but are loved by the vocaloid fans. This further emphasizes the pop-culture aspect of Miku as it shows the reproduction side of pop-culture, where when a company has a successful pop-culture product, they will do anything in their power to create something similar with the same level of popularity. These additional Vocaloids can also be considered Japanese since they were produced at the Yamaha facility in Japan."

Open_Source

"Miku is more than Japanese pop-culture though, she has also had a large impact on open-source culture. The success of Miku can mostly be attributed to, 'amateur self-published fan creations based on famous characters'(Halo, 3). Which is a prime example of open source culture. Open source culture is formed around the concept that information should be free. Before Miku this concept was mostly used in terms of Computer Science, to allow others to alter and fix your code in the name of free exchange. Open source allows people to learn from both the successes and failures of others. However, when Miku started to be used in an open source way, it opened the doors for the open source community. As it became more popular to create your own Miku art based off the open licencing on her image, it became more popular to use the open source mindset on other pieces of art, allowing for the additional creation of pop-art, as well as pop- culture. For example, ever since the copyright for worm on a string died, it has regrown in popularity due to people creating their own worm on a string art, such as making giant worm on a strings."
